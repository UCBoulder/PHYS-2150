## Course Overview

This course is not a typical laboratory course with individual lab experiments. This course is designed
around a real research project to answer new scientific questions. You will work collaboratively with your
classmates, instructors, TAs, technical staff, and an outside science mentor to study a new class of
photovoltaics (solar cells). You will add to the scientific community's knowledge around how the design
of these solar cells contributes to or inhibits degradation under different environmental conditions. The
broader goal of this multi-year project is to help contribute to the efforts to make more efficient and
durable solar cells, which are a key component in sustainable energy production.

## Course Goals

The purpose of the course is to introduce you to the process of experimental research in physics. After successfully completing this course, you should be able to:

- Explain the main components of the research process
- Use Python to do basic experimental data analysis
- Identify a research question and conduct analysis to answer it
- Iterate on proposals based on feedback
- Work collaboratively with a team of students to achieve a common goal
- Measurement Uncertainty concepts and practices
  - Fit a line to experimental data and extract meaning from fit results
  - Determine if two measurements (with uncertainty) agree with each other
  - Propagate uncertainties using formulas
  - Articulate that repeated measurements will give a distribution of results and not a single number
  - Estimate size of random/statistical uncertainty by considering instrument precision
  - Calculate and report the mean of a distribution for the best estimate of the measurement
  - Appropriately use and differentiate between standard deviation and standard error

It is equally important to state what are NOT goals for this class:

- This course is not meant to reinforce physics concepts covered in PHYS 2170.
- This course is not meant to make you a Python programmer.
- This course is not meant to have you understand all of the details of solar cells.

## Lectures

There are several lectures spaced throughout the semester, but most of them are at the beginning of the course. Lectures are in person only and not recorded. Clicker questions will be asked during lectures as an important part of participation.

You will receive one point for answering and an additional point for answering correctly. You must be present in lecture to receive points for clicker questions. Answering on your phone from elsewhere is an honor code violation. For the final grade, the lowest clicker score will be dropped, which includes a missed lecture for any reason. Lecture notes will be posted on Canvas by the end of the week.

## Research Meetings

There are two research meetings this semester, led by the scientific group leader, Dr. Joe Berry. The first meeting presents the larger scientific picture of the project and gives some background information. The second meeting (near the end of the semester) discusses the results of the research study including data and analysis from student teams. These occur during normal lecture times.

## Labs

You must attend the lab section for which you are enrolled to receive credit as you will be working in teams. You will receive credit for *active* lab section participation.

- In general, there are no makeup lab sessions. This is due to the nature of the course which has you work on an authentic research project.
- If you have a University of Colorado sponsored event that conflicts with your lab session, you must contact the Professor (not the TAs) at least one week before your missed section. You will receive no credit for any labs missed with less than one-week notice.
- If you miss a lab section due to illness or emergency, contact the Professor (not the TAs) within one week of missing your lab to request a professor excused absence.
- If you are late to your lab session, your lab participation score may be reduced. This course is team based, so it is important you contribute to the work of your team.

## Policy on the Use of Artificial Intelligence (AI)

In this lab course, students are expected to develop a deep understanding of the scientific and technical principles through hands-on experience, individual, and collaborative efforts.

1. **Permissible Use of AI:**
    - Students are permitted to use AI tools, such as ChatGPT, strictly for Python coding tasks. This includes generating code snippets, debugging, and seeking coding-related advice.
    - Any AI-generated code must be reviewed and understood by the student.

2. **Prohibited Use of AI:**
    - AI tools must not be used for any non-coding related tasks, including but not limited to:
        - Writing lab documents or any written assignments.
        - Generating explanations, analyses, or summaries of lab results.
        - Completing any assessments.
    - Using AI to gather, analyze, or interpret experimental data is not allowed. Students must perform these tasks to ensure they grasp the underlying concepts and methodologies.

3. **Academic Integrity:**
    - Any violation of this policy will be considered a violation of CU's Honor Code.
    - Students must clearly document any use of AI in their coding process, including specifying which tool was used and how it contributed to the code development. There is a section in each Colab notebook for recording this information.

4. **Learning and Support:**
    - The primary goal of this course is to enhance your research knowledge and skills. While AI can be a helpful tool, it should not replace your own learning process.
    - If you have any questions or need assistance with the lab work, please reach out to the instructor or teaching assistants during office hours or lab sessions.
